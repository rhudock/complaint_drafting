complaint drafting application

**Playbook: Drafting a Federal Court Complaint for a Contract Dispute**

1.  **Overview and Key Rules**

Filing a complaint in federal court for a contract dispute requires meeting the basic pleading rules and including specific sections. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure – especially Rule 8 (general pleading standards), Rule 10 (form of pleadings), and Rule 11 (signing and certifications) – set the framework. In essence, a well-drafted complaint must contain:

-   **Caption and case information** (court name, title of the action, and parties – per Rule 10(a)).

-   **Jurisdictional statement** (grounds for subject-matter jurisdiction – Rule 8(a)(1)).

-   **Identification of parties** (who the plaintiff(s) and defendant(s) are, often including their citizenship for jurisdiction).

-   **Statement of the claim** (the facts showing entitlement to relief – Rule 8(a)(2) – in this case, facts supporting a breach of contract).

-   **Demand for relief** (the remedy sought – Rule 8(a)(3)).

-   **Signature block** (attorney or pro se signature, with required details – Rule 11(a)).

Each of these components must satisfy certain legal standards. Rule 8 demands a “short and plain” statement of the claim **showing** entitlement to relief , which, after *Twombly* and *Iqbal*, means the claim must be **plausible** on its face, supported by sufficient factual matter and not merely legal conclusions . Rule 10 prescribes the formal layout (caption, numbered paragraphs, separate counts) . Rule 11 requires signing the complaint and thereby certifying it’s filed in good faith with factual and legal support .

Below is a step-by-step playbook breaking down each element of a federal complaint for breach of contract, with legal standards, common pitfalls, best practices, and sample language.

1.  **Caption and Title (Rule 10(a))**

Every complaint begins with a **caption** at the top of the first page. The caption lists the court name, the title of the action, the docket number (assigned by the clerk; if unknown, leave a blank for it), and a designation as a “Complaint” . In the title of the complaint, **all parties must be named** (Plaintiff(s) v. Defendant(s)) . Key points for the caption:

-   **Court Name:** Identify the correct federal court (e.g., “United States District Court for the [District/Western District of State]”).

-   **Parties:** List each party’s full name. *Rule 10(a)* requires that the title of the complaint include the names of all the parties (at least the first party on each side if many parties, with an “et al.” for others) .

-   **Case Number:** If you know it (for an existing case or if the clerk pre-assigned it), include it. Otherwise, it will be added by the clerk.

-   **Document Title:** Clearly label the document as “Complaint” (and you can add a descriptor like “Complaint for Breach of Contract”). If a jury trial is desired, many practitioners also note “Jury Trial Demanded” in the caption as a best practice (though a jury demand can be made separately per Rule 38).

*Example – Caption:*

United States District Court

Eastern District of Virginia

John Doe, )

Plaintiff, )

)

v. ) Case No. \_______\_

)

ACME Corporation, )

Defendant. )

COMPLAINT (Breach of Contract)

**Pitfalls to avoid:** Ensure all parties are named correctly – spelling errors or omissions can cause confusion. Do not use an ambiguous “et al.” in the complaint’s title for plaintiffs or defendants unless there truly are additional unnamed parties; Rule 10(a) expects all parties to be identified . Also, make sure the caption conforms to any **local court rules** (some districts have specific formatting requirements for captions). Finally, if you intend to request a jury trial, remember to **demand it** in the caption or in a separate section – failing to demand on time may waive the right to a jury.

1.  **Jurisdictional Statement (Rule 8(a)(1))**

The first substantive section of the complaint should state the basis for the federal court’s **subject-matter jurisdiction** . Rule 8(a)(1) mandates “a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court’s jurisdiction” . In a contract dispute, federal jurisdiction commonly exists either through **diversity jurisdiction** or (less often) a **federal question** (e.g. if the contract claim arises under a federal statute or involves the U.S. as a party).

**Include the following:**

-   **Statutory Grounds:** Cite the statute that grants jurisdiction. For example:

    -   *Diversity:* 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a) (diversity jurisdiction, requiring complete diversity of citizenship between plaintiffs and defendants and an amount in controversy exceeding \$75,000).

    -   *Federal Question:* 28 U.S.C. § 1331 (federal question jurisdiction, if the claim arises under federal law).

-   **Diversity Details:** If invoking diversity, **plead the citizenship of each party** and the amount in controversy. This usually means stating the state of citizenship of all individuals, or the state of incorporation and principal place of business for corporations, etc. *Example:* “Plaintiff is a citizen of Virginia, and Defendant ACME Corp. is incorporated in Delaware with its principal place of business in New York. The amount in controversy exceeds \$75,000, exclusive of interest and costs, thus invoking this Court’s diversity jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332(a).”

-   **Federal Question Details:** If invoking a federal question, identify the specific federal law or constitutional provision at issue. (Contract disputes are typically state-law claims, so this is less common unless tied to a federal program or statute.)

-   **Supplemental Jurisdiction:** If your breach of contract claim is accompanied by other state-law claims and you already have a federal anchor claim, you might cite 28 U.S.C. § 1367 for supplemental jurisdiction. But if the contract claim *is* the main claim, then §1332 or §1331 is your focus.

*Sample Jurisdictional Statement:*

**Jurisdiction and Venue:** This Court has subject-matter jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332. The parties are completely diverse: Plaintiff Jane Smith is a citizen of the Commonwealth of Virginia, and Defendant XYZ LLC is a limited liability company whose members are all citizens of Maryland. The amount in controversy exceeds \$75,000, as Plaintiff seeks damages of approximately \$250,000 for breach of contract. Venue is proper in this District under 28 U.S.C. § 1391(b)(2) because a substantial part of the events giving rise to the claim occurred in this District.

Notice the inclusion of a brief **venue** statement here. While Rule 8(a) doesn’t explicitly require pleading venue, it’s common (and good practice) to include a sentence about venue for completeness – referencing the general venue statute, 28 U.S.C. § 1391, and the reasons why the chosen district is appropriate (e.g., where the contract was performed or breached).

**Legal standard:** The jurisdictional statement must be more than a bare conclusion; it should include facts to show jurisdiction is proper. For diversity cases, that means alleging the citizenship of the parties and that the controversy exceeds \$75,000 . Failing to plead these specifics is a common pitfall that can lead to dismissal or an order to amend, because the court cannot assume jurisdiction without it. While the Rule 8 pleading standard (discussed below) primarily concerns the claim’s substantive sufficiency, **jurisdictional allegations** must also be accurate and made in good faith – Rule 11 applies to these statements as well, so do not guess at a party’s citizenship or the amount in controversy without basis.

**Pitfalls:** A common mistake is alleging **residency instead of citizenship** for individuals. Citizenship for diversity means domicile (residence + intent to remain), so state “citizen of [State]” rather than just “resides in [State].” Similarly, for entities, make sure to allege the correct jurisdictional facts (e.g., every member of an LLC, both incorporation and principal place of business for a corporation). Another pitfall is forgetting to state the amount in controversy – simply saying “jurisdiction is proper under 28 U.S.C. § 1332” without more is conclusory and inadequate. Provide the factual basis (even if generally phrased) for why the claim exceeds \$75,000, or explicitly state that it does.

**Best practices:** Keep the jurisdictional statement concise and straightforward (one or two paragraphs). Double-check the diversity allegations for accuracy. If any party’s citizenship is complex (e.g., a trust, partnership, or LLC), research the rules for pleading those. This is one area where attention to detail is critical, because a jurisdictional defect can derail the case at the outset.

1.  **Parties Section**

Although not specifically dictated by Rule 8’s text, a **“Parties” section** is a conventional part of federal complaints. This section identifies each plaintiff and each defendant and provides relevant background such as their status and relationships to the dispute. It serves to tell the court who is involved and, in diversity cases, to bolster the jurisdictional allegations with more detail about citizenship.

**What to include for each party:**

-   **Full legal name and capacity:** For individuals, use their name (and if they are suing or being sued in a special capacity like as executor of an estate, note that). For corporations or organizations, give the full legal name and entity type (e.g., “ACME Corporation, a Delaware corporation”).

-   **Location/citizenship:** State where the party is a citizen or is located. For individuals, their state of citizenship; for corporations, their state of incorporation and principal place of business; for LLCs or partnerships, the citizenship of members/partners (if necessary, or at least principal place of business and state of formation with a note that members are of certain citizenships). *Example:* “Plaintiff ABC Corp. is a corporation organized under the laws of California with its principal place of business in Texas . Defendant XYZ Inc. is incorporated in Delaware and headquartered in New York.” This ties back to diversity jurisdiction requirements.

-   **Role in the dispute:** A brief description of each party’s role or relationship can be helpful. For instance, “ABC Corp. is a widget supplier, and XYZ Inc. is a retailer; the parties entered into a supply contract at issue in this case.” This gives context for the later factual allegations.

-   **If relevant, mention authority:** If a party is a minor, legally incompetent, or an entity that requires a representative, note the representative capacity (e.g., “John Doe, a minor, by his guardian Jane Doe”). Rule 17 deals with capacity, but it’s good to identify these in the Parties section for clarity.

*Sample language:*

**Parties:**

**Plaintiff:** Acme Industries, Inc. (“Acme”), is a Florida corporation with its principal place of business in Tampa, Florida. Acme is in the business of manufacturing custom hardware components.

**Defendant:** Beta Equipment Co. (“Beta”) is a New York corporation with its headquarters and principal place of business in Albany, New York. Beta distributes hardware and contracted with Acme to purchase custom components for resale.

Each paragraph is usually numbered (following Rule 10(b)’s guidance to use **numbered paragraphs**, each limited to a single set of circumstances ). Notice that we introduce shorthand names in parentheses (e.g., “Acme” and “Beta”) – this is a helpful drafting practice to make the complaint readable. After introducing a shorthand, you can use “Plaintiff” or the short name throughout instead of the full name every time.

**Pitfalls:** Do not skip the parties section or assume the caption alone is enough. The caption names the parties, but the Parties section lets you elaborate critical details (like citizenship and roles). Another pitfall is including *too much* irrelevant detail – keep it to what’s pertinent (for instance, you usually don’t need a full company history or personal biography, just what matters for the case and jurisdiction). However, if certain facts about a party matter to the claims (e.g., one party’s licensed status if it’s relevant to enforcement of the contract), you can include that briefly.

**Best practice:** If jurisdiction is based on diversity, **cross-check your Parties section with your jurisdictional statement** to ensure consistency and completeness. Also, write each party description clearly and factually – these are often some of the first paragraphs the judge will read, so clarity is key. Use separate paragraphs for each party to comply with Rule 10(b)’s requirement of one set of circumstances per paragraph .

1.  **Statement of Facts / Claim (Rule 8(a)(2))**

After setting out the parties and jurisdiction, the complaint should contain a **statement of the claim** – in other words, the factual narrative that shows the plaintiff is entitled to relief . Rule 8(a)(2) famously requires “a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief” . This section is often introduced with a heading like “Facts” or “Factual Background.” It should **tell the story of the contract and its breach in a clear, concise manner.**

**Legal standard – the Twombly/Iqbal plausibility requirement:** Gone are the days of extremely bare-bones notice pleading where a complaint could survive with conclusory assertions. The U.S. Supreme Court in *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly* and *Ashcroft v. Iqbal* established that a complaint must allege **enough facts** to state a claim that is plausible on its face . This does **not** mean a complaint needs to plead detailed evidence or meet a probability standard, but it must contain more than labels, conclusions, or a formulaic recitation of elements . The court will disregard purely conclusory allegations and examine whether the well-pleaded facts (assumed true) **plausibly** suggest a right to relief . In practical terms, you should:

-   Include **factual context** (who, what, when, where, how) for each element of the claim.

-   Avoid or explain **legal conclusions**. It’s fine to use legal terms (like “breach” or “damages”), but they should be supported by specific facts (e.g., how the breach occurred, the nature of damages).

-   Strive for a narrative that would make sense to someone unfamiliar with the dispute – it should give “fair notice” of what happened and why it’s a breach of contract .

*Twombly* emphasized that while Rule 8 does not require detailed facts, it **does require** enough facts to raise the right to relief above a speculative level . The complaint must “nudge[] the[] claims across the line from conceivable to plausible” . *Iqbal* reaffirmed that **threadbare recitals of the elements** of a cause of action, **supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice** . Thus, in your facts section, make sure each key allegation is concrete and factual. For example, instead of saying “Defendant breached the contract,” say **how** the defendant breached: e.g., “Defendant failed to deliver the 500 units by the August 1, 2025 deadline, as required by Section 2 of the contract.” This grounds the legal conclusion (“breach”) in factual detail.

**What to cover in a contract dispute’s facts section:**

-   **Existence of a Contract:** Allege when and how the contract was formed. Specify the date of the agreement, the parties to the contract, and the general nature of the contract (e.g., a sales agreement, service contract, lease, etc.). If it was written, you might state “The parties entered into a written contract on May 1, 2025, titled ‘Supply Agreement’.” If the contract is oral or partly oral, you must plead the fact of the agreement and its key terms (keeping in mind the Statute of Frauds if applicable).

    -   **Tip:** If the contract is written, **attach it as an exhibit** and incorporate it by reference. Under Rule 10(c), an exhibit that is a written instrument (like a contract) is treated as part of the pleading . Attaching the contract (as **Exhibit A**) and stating “a true and correct copy of the contract is attached as Exhibit A” both strengthens the plausibility of the claim and helps avoid disputes about what the contract says.

-   **Plaintiff’s Performance (or Excuse):** To show entitlement to relief for breach, it’s usually necessary to allege that the plaintiff **performed its obligations** under the contract, or was excused from performance. For example, “Plaintiff fully performed all required conditions, including paying the purchase price in advance.” If some condition precedent was required (like giving notice or an opportunity to cure), allege that those **conditions precedent** have been satisfied or waived. (Per Rule 9(c), conditions precedent may be pleaded generally – e.g., “All conditions precedent to Defendant’s performance have occurred or been satisfied” – which is a common line to include in contract complaints.)

-   **Defendant’s Breach:** Identify **what provision or duty** the defendant failed to perform and **how**. Be specific: “Defendant breached the contract by [doing X]/[failing to do Y] in violation of Section \_\_ of the agreement.” For instance, “On August 2, 2025, Defendant breached the Supply Agreement by failing to deliver any of the 500 units due, despite Plaintiff’s timely payment.” If the breach is of a particular clause, it’s a best practice (especially in some jurisdictions) to **identify the contract provision** that was breached . The New York courts, for example, require plaintiffs to identify the specific term of the contract that was breached . While federal notice-pleading is more lenient, being specific here makes the claim stronger and more immune to a motion to dismiss. (A vague allegation like “Defendant didn’t do what was promised” is risky – it may be challenged as too conclusory or insufficient.)

-   **Resulting Damages:** State clearly that the plaintiff was injured or incurred losses because of the breach. This can be a brief statement of the harm and an approximate dollar amount or other relief sought. For example: “As a direct result of Defendant’s breach, Plaintiff suffered \$200,000 in losses, consisting of lost profits and the costs of securing replacement goods.” You don’t have to prove the amount in the complaint, but you should assert that you **have been damaged** and give a sense of scale or nature of damages (especially since in contract law, nominal damages can be available – but if you only plead nominal harm, the court might question the value of the case).

These factual allegations should be arranged in **numbered paragraphs** (Rule 10(b) requires numbered paragraphs, each limited to a single set of circumstances) . Chronological order is usually effective: start with the formation of the contract, then performance, then breach, then aftermath/damages. If multiple distinct breaches or multiple contracts are involved, consider breaking them into sub-sections or separate counts for clarity (Rule 10(b) actually advises that each claim founded on a separate transaction or occurrence *must* be stated in a separate count if it promotes clarity ).

**Example – Facts section snippet:**

**Factual Background:**

1.  *Formation of Contract.* On May 1, 2025, Plaintiff Acme Industries, Inc. and Defendant Beta Equipment Co. entered into a written **Supply Agreement** (the “Agreement”) for the sale of 1,000 custom-fabricated widgets. A copy of the Agreement is attached as **Exhibit A** . Under the Agreement, Acme agreed to manufacture and deliver 1,000 widgets to Beta by August 1, 2025, and Beta agreed to pay Acme \$500,000 (50% upfront and 50% on delivery).

2.  *Plaintiff’s Performance.* Acme timely performed all of its obligations. Acme completed production of the 1,000 widgets and delivered them to Beta’s Albany warehouse on July 30, 2025. Acme also fulfilled all **conditions precedent** under the contract (including providing a certificate of quality and an invoice) .

3.  *Defendant’s Breach.* Beta accepted delivery of the widgets, but **failed to pay** the remaining \$250,000 due on August 1, 2025, in breach of Section 5 of the Agreement. Beta’s officer informed Acme on August 15, 2025 that Beta “will not be sending payment,” which repudiated Beta’s payment obligation.

4.  *Damages.* As a result of Beta’s breach, Acme has suffered \$250,000 in direct damages (the unpaid contract price), plus incidental damages including storage costs and financing expenses. Acme continues to incur interest on the unpaid amount.

This example illustrates clear, numbered factual allegations that correspond to each element of a breach of contract claim. It names the contract, references an exhibit, states performance, identifies the breach (non-payment), and quantifies damages. It also avoids legal argument – it’s purely factual, saving any overt legal conclusions for the later “Count” section (though even in the count, the allegations should remain factual in form).

**Plausibility check:** After drafting the facts, re-read them and ask: *Do these facts, assumed true, show a plausible claim for breach of contract?* The judge should be able to see that if these facts are true, the law would entitle you to relief. Under *Twombly*/*Iqbal*, for a simple contract case, relatively straightforward factual allegations usually suffice . In fact, courts recognize that breach of contract claims typically **don’t require extensive detail** to be plausible – you just need the basic contract and breach facts. However, **missing any element** (e.g., failing to allege the existence of a contract or a breach) will make the claim implausible. One common mistake is pleading that the defendant breached, but not making clear that there was a valid contract or that the plaintiff held up their end. Make sure the factual story is complete and coherent.

**Common pitfalls in the facts/claim section:**

-   **Conclusory allegations:** Saying “Defendant breached the contract and caused damages” with no supporting facts is inadequate . Always back up conclusions with concrete examples or descriptions.

-   **Overpleading or irrelevancies:** While detail is good, avoid rambling or including every minor grievance if it’s not directly tied to the breach. Stick to material facts that pertain to the claim. Irrelevant facts can confuse the issues or even harm your case if they admit unnecessary points.

-   **Inconsistencies:** Ensure that the facts you allege don’t contradict each other or other documents. Under Rule 11, you certify that you have or are likely to have evidentiary support for factual allegations , so don’t include speculative facts that you have no basis for. If you must plead in the alternative (e.g., you’re not sure which of two scenarios the evidence will support), Rule 8(d)(2) allows alternative statements of a claim , but make it clear (for instance: “In the alternative, if the contract is deemed void, then Plaintiff pleads unjust enrichment…” etc.).

-   **Forgetting exhibits:** If the contract is central and especially if it’s short, attach it. Not attaching a key written contract isn’t fatal, but the defendant might file a motion for a more definite statement or move to dismiss arguing the terms weren’t adequately pleaded. Exhibits can preempt that. Remember that per Rule 10(c), an attached written contract becomes part of the pleading , and the court can consider it on a motion to dismiss without converting to summary judgment, since it’s integral to the complaint.

**Best practices:** Write the factual narrative in plain language. Use simple, direct sentences (Rule 8(d)(1) requires each allegation to be “simple, concise, and direct” ). It’s acceptable and often helpful to break the facts into paragraphs that correspond with the elements of the claim. For example, you might have a paragraph or set of paragraphs under a mini-heading “The Contract,” another under “Plaintiff’s Performance,” “Breach by Defendant,” and “Damages.” This kind of organization is reader-friendly and ensures you cover everything necessary.

Before finalizing, cross-check the facts against the legal elements of **breach of contract** (detailed in the next section). Each element should be supported by at least one factual allegation.

1.  **Stating the Breach of Contract Claim (Legal Elements and Standard)**

After laying out the facts, it’s typical (though not strictly required) to include a section explicitly labeling the **cause of action** – often called a “Count.” In a simple case with one cause of action, some complaints merge the factual allegations and the claim, but it’s clearer to separate them. For example:

**“Count I – Breach of Contract”** (against [Defendant’s Name]) might be a heading after the factual background. Under that heading, you can incorporate the previous facts and explicitly assert that those facts constitute a breach of contract by the defendant.

**Elements of a breach of contract claim:** While the exact phrasing can vary by jurisdiction, generally the complaint must show these elements :

1.  **Existence of a valid and enforceable contract** between the parties.

2.  **Plaintiff’s performance** of its obligations under the contract (or a valid excuse for non-performance).

3.  **Defendant’s breach** of the contract (an unjustified failure to perform a material obligation).

4.  **Damages** caused by the breach (or some recognized harm, even if nominal).

Federal courts will apply the contract law of the relevant state (in a diversity case), but these core elements are common to contract claims in most jurisdictions . The *pleading* standard does not require citing the law or reciting the elements verbatim; instead, you must allege facts that, if true, satisfy those elements.

**Legal standard for each element:**

-   **Contract Existence:** The complaint should make it **plausible** that there was an agreement. This usually means alleging offer, acceptance, and consideration in some form. If the contract was written, identifying the written document (and attaching it) suffices to show an agreement existed. If oral, you may need a bit more detail (e.g., the contract’s main terms and that it was agreed on a certain date). Conclusory statements like “the parties had a contract” are bolstered by factual context (e.g., “the parties agreed that X would do Y and Z would pay \$N”). Note that under Rule 8, you do **not** have to anticipate affirmative defenses like Statute of Frauds or argue enforceability in the complaint (unless the contract is obviously of a type that must be written, in which case you should ensure you’ve indicated it was written or an exception applies).

-   **Plaintiff’s Performance:** A short statement that the plaintiff fulfilled its obligations (or was ready, willing, and able to perform, or was excused) is important. If the plaintiff did not perform a certain obligation, the complaint should preemptively state any excuse or waiver (to avoid the impression of the plaintiff being in breach). Under Rule 9(c), conditions precedent can be alleged generally , so it’s sufficient to say “Plaintiff has performed all of its duties and conditions precedent required under the contract.” This element assures the court that the plaintiff isn’t suing despite their own material breach or failure to perform.

-   **Breach by Defendant:** This is the core wrongful act – it must be described clearly. The legal standard for breach in pleading is simply to give notice of what the breach was. There’s no need to prove it at this stage, but you should allege facts showing how the defendant failed to meet their obligations. If multiple breaches or multiple provisions breached, consider separating them (e.g., “Defendant breached the contract in the following ways: (a) \_\_ ; (b) \__.”). **Materiality** of the breach (if relevant under state law) can be implied by the nature of the breach (failing to pay or deliver is inherently material). If a breach was minor but you’re still suing, you might need to address materiality or resulting harm explicitly so it doesn’t appear trivial.

-   **Damages:** You must state that as a result of the breach, plaintiff suffered damages. The law typically requires damages (or at least nominal damages) for a breach of contract claim to be actionable. In pleading, you don’t need an exact calculation, but you should assert the type of damages (money lost, costs incurred, lost profits, etc.) and that they were caused by the breach. For example: “Plaintiff incurred \$50,000 in extra costs to cover because Defendant’s breach left it without the goods.” If seeking specific performance or injunctive relief instead of or in addition to money, note the harm that justifies that remedy.

**Sample – Count for Breach of Contract:**

**Count I – Breach of Contract (Against Beta Equipment Co.)**

1.  **Incorporation of Allegations.** Plaintiff Acme incorporates by reference all the above paragraphs as if fully set forth herein (Rule 10(c)) .

2.  **Existence of Contract.** Acme and Beta entered into a valid, written contract (the “Supply Agreement”) on May 1, 2025, whereby Acme agreed to manufacture 1,000 widgets and Beta agreed to pay \$500,000 (half in advance, half on delivery).

3.  **Plaintiff’s Performance.** Acme has **performed all of its obligations** under the Supply Agreement, as described above, or performance has been excused or waived.

4.  **Defendant’s Breach.** Beta materially **breached** the Supply Agreement by failing to pay the remaining \$250,000 due, and by repudiating its payment obligation without justification.

5.  **Damages.** As a direct and proximate result of Beta’s breach, Acme has suffered damages in an amount to be proven at trial, currently estimated at \$250,000, plus interest, attorneys’ fees (as provided by the contract), and other costs.

6.  **Relief Sought.** (See Prayer for Relief below.)

This breakdown uses separate paragraphs for clarity and labels each element (this is optional, but a good way to ensure you hit everything). Paragraph 5 pulls in the facts already stated, so you don’t repeat all the details again. The subsequent paragraphs explicitly tie the facts to the legal elements: contract formation, performance, breach, and damages. This method demonstrates to the court that you understand what must be shown, and it makes the complaint read like a checklist of the cause of action. It also makes it easier for the defendant (and the court) to see exactly what is being claimed and which facts support which element – potentially dissuading the defendant from moving to dismiss, since the claim’s sufficiency is evident.

**Pitfalls:** One pitfall is not clearly incorporating prior allegations, which can lead to confusion about whether the earlier facts are part of the claim. Use a sentence to incorporate prior factual allegations into each count (or you can re-allege specific paragraphs by number). Another pitfall is **mislabeling or mixing claims** – for instance, labeling something “Breach of Contract” but then also throwing in allegations of fraud or some other tort in the same count. Keep each cause of action separate and clearly labeled. If you have multiple claims (e.g., Breach of Contract and an alternative Unjust Enrichment), each should be its own count.

Also, be careful with **demanding attorneys’ fees or special damages** in the count: Generally, under the American Rule, attorneys’ fees are not recoverable in contract cases unless the contract or a statute provides for them. If your contract has a fees clause, you can plead that as part of damages (as in the sample above). If not, don’t include fees in damages or you might get a motion to strike. Similarly, if you have any *special damages* (consequential damages that are not the usual result of breach, like lost business opportunities), Rule 9(g) says they should be specifically stated. For example, if a breached contract caused you to lose a major resale deal, explicitly mention that loss in the damages to ensure it’s not considered waived .

**Best practices:** Keep the breach of contract count focused. Use the count to ensure **each legal element is addressed**. You don’t necessarily need to cite case law within the complaint (complaints usually don’t cite cases; they rely on facts and the court applies the law). But you *can* refer to specific clauses of the contract or statutory provisions if relevant (e.g., “pursuant to Va. Code § \__, Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees”). However, detailed legal arguments and case citations are typically reserved for memoranda, not the complaint itself. The goal is a clean, factual pleading that could stand on its own to show a valid claim.

1.  **Demand for Relief (Rule 8(a)(3))**

After stating the cause(s) of action, the complaint must include a **demand for judgment** – commonly called the “Prayer for Relief.” Rule 8(a)(3) requires “a demand for the relief sought, which may include relief in the alternative or different types of relief” . This section, often introduced by “Wherefore” or simply under a heading “Prayer for Relief,” tells the court what you want if you prevail.

**Key components of a demand for relief:**

-   **Monetary Damages:** State the types of damages you seek. In a contract case, this usually includes **compensatory damages** (the amount to compensate for the breach – e.g., expectation damages or reliance damages). You can specify an amount or state it will be determined at trial, but note that some federal courts prefer you not to state a specific dollar figure if it’s substantial, saying instead “in an amount to be proven at trial, but in excess of \$X” or “in excess of the jurisdictional minimum.” Check local practice – some jurisdictions (like New York state) prohibit stating an amount in certain cases, but federal courts generally allow it. In diversity cases, it can be wise to at least assert that the amount exceeds \$75,000 to reinforce jurisdiction.

-   **Interest:** If you seek prejudgment interest (often allowed in contract cases as either statutory or as provided in the contract), include that. E.g., “plus pre-judgment interest at the legal rate from August 1, 2025 until judgment.”

-   **Attorneys’ Fees and Costs:** If applicable, request attorneys’ fees (but only if you have a basis – such as a contract clause or a statute) and court costs (which are usually granted to the prevailing party by rule, but it’s fine to ask for them). E.g., “Plaintiff further requests an award of its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs incurred herein, to the extent permitted by law or contract.”

-   **Equitable Relief:** If you want something other than money – like specific performance (common in unique goods or real estate contracts), or an injunction – list it. For example, “an order requiring Defendant to specifically perform the contract and deliver the 1907 antique automobile to Plaintiff.” Under Rule 8(a)(3) you can demand relief in the **alternative** , so you might say “Plaintiff seeks, **in the alternative**, specific performance of the contract if monetary damages are inadequate to make Plaintiff whole.”

-   **Declaratory Relief:** If applicable, you could ask for a declaratory judgment (28 U.S.C. § 2201) clarifying the parties’ rights under the contract, though in a pure breach case, that’s less common unless you also have an ongoing contract and need interpretation for future performance.

-   **“Such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper”:** This catch-all phrase is almost always included to ensure you’re not limited to the enumerated relief if the court thinks something else is appropriate.

The demand can be in either prose or bullet/numbered form. A common format is a bulleted or numbered list following a lead-in sentence.

*Example – Prayer for Relief:*

**WHEREFORE**, Plaintiff Acme Industries, Inc. respectfully requests that the Court enter judgment in its favor and grant the following relief:

-   **Compensatory Damages:** An award of damages against Defendant Beta Equipment Co. in the amount of \$250,000, or an amount to be determined at trial, for breach of contract;

-   **Prejudgment Interest:** An award of pre-judgment interest on the above damages at the rate of 6% per annum from August 1, 2025 until the date of judgment (or at such rate and from such date as determined by law);

-   **Attorneys’ Fees and Costs:** An award of Acme’s reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs of suit, as provided by Section 10 of the Supply Agreement (or as otherwise allowed by law); and

-   **Other Relief:** Such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper .

This example shows a structured demand including the main categories of relief. It cites the contract for fees – if you have no contractual or statutory basis for fees, omit that bullet or you might invite a motion to strike. The catch-all “such other and further relief…” ensures flexibility.

**Legal considerations:** Under Rule 54(c), the final judgment can grant any relief to which the party is entitled, **even if not explicitly demanded in the complaint**, except for a default judgment (where relief is limited to what was demanded). That means you won’t necessarily be strictly held to the exact figures or types of relief in your complaint, but you should still be as comprehensive as possible in the demand. For instance, if you forget to demand specific performance, you might still argue for it later if the law allows, but it’s better to put all conceivable relief in the complaint.

One thing to note is that punitive damages are generally **not available** for pure breach of contract (unless some independent tort or bad faith is involved under specific law). So in a standard contract dispute, **do not demand punitive damages** – it will look like a legal error and could undermine your credibility. Only include categories of damages that are actually recoverable for your claim.

**Pitfalls:** A surprisingly common mistake is **forgetting the demand for relief entirely** – don’t let the rush of writing the factual sections make you miss this required element. Another pitfall is being too vague (e.g., just saying “Plaintiff demands judgment in its favor” without specifying what that judgment should entail). While you don’t have to list a sum certain for unliquidated damages, you should outline the components of relief. On the flip side, avoid overly detailed damage calculations in the complaint – you can leave the exact numbers to proof. Listing every penny of damage or itemizing dozens of things can clutter the pleading. Stick to broad categories (like “lost profits of approximately \$X” or “costs of cover in an amount to be proven at trial”).

Be cautious about demanding remedies that are inconsistent – although Rule 8(d)(2) allows pleading in the alternative, you should clarify if remedies are in the alternative. For example, don’t demand both full damages and specific performance for the same breach without explaining one is alternative, because you normally can’t recover both (specific performance or damages).

**Best practices:** Tailor the prayer for relief to the case. Judges often glance at the wherefore clause to understand what the plaintiff wants. Make it easy to read – a bulleted or numbered list as above is ideal. Ensure the relief aligns with the claims (e.g., if you mentioned attorneys’ fees in the contract, include them; if not, including them could be a red flag). If you have multiple counts, you can either do a single combined prayer at the end covering all counts, or separate prayers per count. A single consolidated prayer is usually simpler.

Finally, even though the demand is the last substantive part, don’t treat it as a throwaway – it’s literally what you’re asking the court for, so give it a careful look to make sure everything is included and properly stated.

1.  **Signature and Rule 11 Certification (Rule 11(a) and (b))**

Every pleading filed with the court **must be signed** by at least one attorney of record, or by the party personally if unrepresented (pro se) . The signature block usually appears at the end of the complaint (after the prayer for relief), and it should include the signer’s address, email, and telephone number . By signing, you are making certain certifications under Rule 11(b) – essentially that the complaint isn’t being filed for an improper purpose, and that your legal claims and factual allegations have a non-frivolous basis .

**Signature block format:**

Typically, it looks like:

Dated: January 9, 2026 Respectfully submitted,

/s/ Jane Q. Lawyer

Jane Q. Lawyer (Va. Bar \#12345)

XYZ Law Firm PLLC

123 Main Street, Suite 100

Richmond, VA 23219

Telephone: (804) 555-1234

Email: jlawyer@xyzfirm.com

Counsel for Plaintiff Acme Industries, Inc.

If you’re filing pro se, you’d sign and then print your name, address, etc., and note that you are self-represented.

**Rule 11 specifics:** Rule 11(a) requires the signature and contact information and states that the court must strike an unsigned paper unless promptly corrected . So it’s critical not to forget the signature! Rule 11(b) spells out the certification: by signing or filing, you certify that to the best of your knowledge, after reasonable inquiry, (1) the paper is not for an **improper purpose** (harassment, unnecessary delay, etc.); (2) the **legal contentions** are warranted by existing law or a nonfrivolous argument to change the law; (3) the **factual contentions** have evidentiary support (or likely will after discovery); and (4) denials of factual contentions (if any) are warranted on the evidence or lack of information . In a complaint, prongs (3) and (1) are particularly in focus – basically, you must have or reasonably expect to have proof for the facts you allege, and you’re not suing just to harass the defendant.

**Practical tips for Rule 11 compliance:**

-   Before signing, double-check every factual allegation for a good-faith basis. Do you have documents, witness statements, or personal knowledge for it, or at least a strong reason to believe it will have support in discovery? If not, consider toning it down or omitting it.

-   Ensure your legal theory (breach of contract in this case) is **warranted by existing law**. A standard breach of contract claim certainly is, as long as you have a valid contract and breach. If you were attempting some novel contract theory, you’d want to ensure you cite some legal authority (in the complaint or more likely later in briefing) to show it’s not frivolous.

-   Check that you haven’t included any obviously **improper matter** – like irrelevant personal attacks on the defendant, or scandalous accusations unrelated to the claim. Rule 11 doesn’t allow using pleadings to vent or embarrass; everything should be aimed at pursuing a proper legal claim. If something is inflammatory but relevant (e.g. defendant’s fraudulent intent, if it were a fraud case), it’s allowed, but in a straight breach of contract, you usually can keep the tone professional and focus on the breach.

**No need for verification (usually):** Rule 11(a) also notes that **pleadings generally do not need to be verified** or accompanied by an affidavit, unless a specific statute or rule requires it . In federal practice, complaints are typically not verified (unlike some state practices or specific types of cases like some admiralty claims or shareholder derivative suits where verification is required by rule). So unless your situation is unusual, you don’t need to have the plaintiff sign a notarized verification. Simply the attorney’s signature (or the party’s, pro se) is sufficient. The signature can be electronic (“/s/ Name” for ECF filing) as long as it complies with the court’s electronic filing rules.

**Pitfalls:** Forgetting to sign is the biggest one – it can happen if you’re rushing, but the court clerk or electronic filing system usually flags it. If it happens, Rule 11(a) gives you a chance to fix it promptly , but avoid the embarrassment by always doing a final check for a signature. Another pitfall is having outdated contact info or missing info in the signature block (some courts will send notices to the address/email on the pleading, so make sure it’s correct).

Under Rule 11(c), if a complaint is frivolous or filed in bad faith, the court (after giving you a chance to respond) can impose **sanctions**, which could range from fines to paying the other side’s legal fees . While this is relatively rare for initial complaints (courts give leeway as long as there’s some arguable basis), be mindful not to include any claim or allegation that isn’t reasonably supported. For example, don’t add a fraud claim to your contract case with no evidence, hoping to scare the defendant, as that could violate Rule 11(b)(3) and (b)(2) (lacking evidentiary support and not warranted by law for the purpose intended).

**Best practice:** Many attorneys will include a final line in the complaint like “Dated: [date]” and then the signature block, as shown above, to make it formal. Also, ensure that if you are representing a client, you have the client’s name correctly listed under “Counsel for [Plaintiff].” If multiple attorneys or a law firm are involved, list them as per local rules (some require listing the bar number, etc., as shown in the sample).

Double-check local court rules as well for any additional required statements – e.g., some courts require a civil cover sheet, or a disclosure statement (Rule 7.1 requires a disclosure of organizational interests for non-governmental corporate parties – usually filed separately but sometimes included). These are not part of the complaint text, but part of the filing packet.

1.  **Common Pitfalls and Best Practices Recap**

To summarize, here are **common pitfalls to avoid** and **best practices to follow** when drafting a federal complaint for breach of contract:

-   **✔️ DO** follow the *“short and plain statement”* ethos of Rule 8 – be concise but include all necessary facts. Every required element (jurisdiction, contract existence, breach, etc.) should have some factual support in the complaint.

-   **❌ DON’T** rely on outdated standards. The old Conley v. Gibson notion that a complaint is sufficient unless “no set of facts” could support the claim is no longer the law – Twombly explicitly retired that standard . Instead, ensure your claim meets the plausibility standard (enough facts to raise a reasonable expectation that discovery will reveal evidence of breach ). Don’t plead mere legal conclusions without facts – e.g., “Defendant breached and caused damages” is not enough without explaining the breach .

-   **✔️ DO** organize the complaint logically. Use headings for each section (as we did in this playbook) if it’s lengthy, or at least clearly separate paragraphs for different topics (jurisdiction, parties, facts, etc.). Numbered paragraphs are required and also helpful for reference . You want the reader (judge or opposing counsel) to be able to skim and understand the gist quickly. Clarity and structure can also lend credibility.

-   **❌ DON’T** include extraneous matters. Irrelevant background, hyperbolic statements, or personal attacks not relevant to the legal claim have no place in a well-pleaded complaint. They can undermine the persuasive force of your legitimate allegations and may run afoul of Rule 11(b)(1)’s prohibition on improper purpose or harassment . Keep it professional and focused on the contract dispute.

-   **✔️ DO** double-check **Federal Rules references**:

    -   Rule 8(a) for the trio of complaint requirements (jurisdiction, claim showing entitlement, demand for relief) .

    -   Rule 8(d) for the directive that allegations be simple, concise, and direct (and note you can plead alternative theories) .

    -   Rule 10 for the form (caption naming all parties, numbered paragraphs, and attaching exhibits) .

    -   Rule 11 for signing and the need for a good-faith basis .

-   **❌ DON’T** forget to review the **substantive law of the governing state** for any unique pleading requirements. Federal rules govern procedure, but the elements of the contract claim come from state law. Some states might, for example, require pleading the specific provision breached (as in the New York example) or might require notice of certain types of damages. While not usually mandatory under federal notice pleading, aligning with those requirements strengthens your complaint against a motion to dismiss.

-   **✔️ DO** include **examples of compliant language** (as we have in this guide) when in doubt. Phrasing like “Plaintiff has performed all conditions precedent” or “Defendant materially breached by doing X” are time-tested ways to cover the bases. If you have access to form books or prior cases, it helps to see how a successful complaint is phrased. Just ensure any form language is tailored to **your facts** – don’t accidentally plead facts that don’t apply to your case by copying a template too closely.

-   **❌ DON’T** overlook the **demand for relief** or the signature. These are simple but essential. A missing demand might not automatically void a complaint (court could allow amendment), but it’s a sign of carelessness. A missing signature will be caught and must be remedied . Always do a final check: caption, jurisdiction, parties, facts, claim, relief, signature – all present and correct.

-   **✔️ DO** keep in mind possible **verification or special requirements** if any. While generally not needed (and indeed Rule 11 says pleadings need not be verified unless required ), certain situations like suing the U.S. government may require a verified complaint by statute, or certain state law attachments (e.g., an affidavit of merit in a malpractice claim, though that’s not contract). For a straightforward contract dispute, these likely don’t apply, but always consider if any special rule (perhaps a local court rule) imposes something extra.

Following this playbook will help ensure your federal court complaint for breach of contract is well-structured, complies with the Federal Rules, and sets forth a compelling, plausible claim. It will contain all mandatory components – from the jurisdictional statement to the signature – and meet the legal standards for each. A strong complaint not only avoids early dismissal but also positions you favorably in the litigation by conveying professionalism and preparedness.

This case retired the “no set of facts” standard and imposed the plausibility threshold .

-   *Ashcroft v. Iqbal*, 556 U.S. 662 (2009) – affirmed that Rule 8 requires factual content to support a plausible claim in **all civil cases**, not just complex ones . Emphasized that “threadbare recitals of the elements of a cause of action, supported by mere conclusory statements, do not suffice” . A claim is plausible when the facts alleged allow the court to draw a reasonable inference of liability .

-   *Speedfit LLC v. Woodway USA, Inc.*, 53 F. Supp. 3d 561, 579 (E.D.N.Y. 2014) – noted that relatively **simple allegations can suffice to plead a breach of contract** even after Twombly/Iqbal , reflecting that straightforward contract claims don’t require extensive detail as long as the basic elements are covered.

-   **Federal Rules Commentary & Practice Guides:** Wright & Miller, *Federal Practice and Procedure*, § 1216 (describing the requirement of stating circumstances, occurrences, and events in support of the claim rather than bare conclusions) . This supports pleading underlying facts and not just a “bare averment” of entitlement to relief .

App. Div. 2009) – requiring plaintiffs to identify the specific contract terms breached . While not binding in federal court outside New York, it illustrates a best practice: identify what part of the contract was broken, so the defendant and court know exactly the basis of the claim .
